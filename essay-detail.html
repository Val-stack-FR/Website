Essay № 01 AI · change management

Why AI adoption stalls:
a field diagnosis

Most AI adoption failures aren't technical. They're organisational. Here's what I've observed over two years working at the intersection of AI strategy and change management.

Most companies approach AI adoption as an engineering problem. They hire data scientists, buy tools, stand up pilots. Then nothing scales. The pilot ends. The team disbands. Eighteen months later, they do it again.[1]

This is the pattern I see most often in the field. And after watching it repeat across sectors — manufacturing, professional services, public institutions — I'm convinced the failure mode isn't technical.

"AI systems require something most organisations have systematically optimised away: tolerance for ambiguity in workflow design."
— Field observation, Ayming Innovation · 2024–2025

The actual bottleneck is organisational

Traditional process improvement — six sigma, lean, ISO certification — rewards the elimination of variation. AI adoption requires reintroducing it, deliberately, at specific joints in the process. The two impulses are structurally opposed.

The organisations that move fluidly into AI aren't the ones with the best data infrastructure. They're the ones where middle management has learned to hold two contradictory things simultaneously: clear accountability structures and undefined process edges.[2]

What freezing looks like

The freeze isn't dramatic. There's no refusal. There's enthusiasm, even — workshops, roadmaps, an AI committee. What's missing is the willingness to let a process be partially broken while it's being redesigned.

So the pilot runs in parallel with the old system, never replacing it. Costs double. The AI system atrophies from lack of real data. Eventually it's quietly retired.

"The pilot runs in parallel with the old system, never replacing it. The AI atrophies from lack of real data."
— Pattern observed across 6 enterprise pilots, 2024

A diagnostic heuristic

One question I ask early: Who is allowed to make a mistake that affects a client, and what happens to them?[3]

In organisations where the answer is "no one, and it's catastrophic", AI adoption will stall at the pilot stage. Every time. The error-handling culture has to change before the tooling can.

This isn't a comfortable finding. It implicates leadership decisions made years before anyone thought about AI. But it's actionable — which is the point.
