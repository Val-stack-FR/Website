Jonathan Haidt's central thesis is simple and hard to argue with: moral judgements are not conclusions reached by reasoning. They are intuitions that arrive instantaneously, and reasoning is deployed afterwards, mostly to justify what we already felt.

This matters enormously for anyone who works in change management, strategy, or communications. If you believe your colleagues are making bad decisions because they haven't heard the right arguments, you will keep being wrong about how to help them.

<div class="pull-quote">
  <blockquote>"The rider is the conscious, verbal, reasoning part of the mind. The elephant is the other 99 percent of mental processes — the ones that actually drive behaviour."</blockquote>
  <cite>— Jonathan Haidt, The Righteous Mind</cite>
</div>

## The moral foundations model

The second half of the book extends the argument: different people don't just have different opinions, they have different moral *foundations* — different things they care about at the most basic level. Care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty. Conservative and progressive politics, Haidt argues, are not two positions on a single spectrum but two different weightings of these foundations.

Whether you find this framework convincing (I find it partially convincing, more as a diagnostic than a normative account), the observation it's built on is correct: most political disagreement is not about facts. It's about which moral foundations you're optimising for.

## Why it matters for organisations

The organisational applications are more interesting than the political ones. Teams fail not because they disagree about data, but because they have different foundational commitments — to efficiency versus loyalty, to innovation versus stability, to individual accountability versus collective responsibility.

Haidt gives you a framework for diagnosing this that's more actionable than "people disagree." And the rider/elephant metaphor is one of the most useful I've encountered for explaining why rational argument is rarely sufficient for changing minds.
