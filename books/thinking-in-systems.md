Donella Meadows wrote this book as a practical guide to systems thinking — a way of seeing the world in terms of stocks, flows, feedback loops, and delays rather than linear cause-and-effect. It's the best introduction to the subject I've found.

What makes it work is the combination of rigour and accessibility. Meadows doesn't talk down to the reader, but she also doesn't hide behind jargon. The examples move from thermostats to ecosystems to arms races without losing the thread.

<div class="pull-quote">
  <blockquote>"A system is more than the sum of its parts. It may exhibit adaptive, dynamic, goal-seeking, self-preserving, and sometimes evolutionary behaviour."</blockquote>
  <cite>— Donella Meadows, Thinking in Systems</cite>
</div>

## The core insight

The reason most interventions fail — in organisations, in policy, in personal behaviour — is that they push on the wrong leverage point. Systems have their own logic, and that logic frequently produces outcomes that are the opposite of what any individual actor intended.

Meadows gives you a vocabulary for diagnosing this: where are the feedback loops? What are the delays? Where is the leverage? Once you have that vocabulary, you start seeing it everywhere — in why AI adoption stalls, in why cost-cutting exercises increase costs, in why good intentions produce bad policies.

## Why I keep returning to it

This is one of those books I recommend to almost everyone I work with in strategy and change management. Not because they'll necessarily read it — most won't — but because the five-minute summary of the core concepts changes how people approach problems immediately.

The idea that you should look for circular causality rather than linear causality, and that delay is often the source of instability rather than bad intention, is worth far more than the cover price.
