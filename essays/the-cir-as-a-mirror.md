Every year, thousands of French companies claim the Crédit Impôt Recherche. The process is bureaucratic, adversarial in places, and often dreaded by R&D teams. But the questions it forces you to answer are among the most clarifying I've encountered in strategy work.

*What, exactly, are you trying to learn? How do you know when you've learned it? What would falsify your hypothesis?*

These are not tax questions. They are the questions of scientific method — and most organisations, when pushed, cannot answer them cleanly about their own innovation work.<span class="fn">[1]</span>

  <blockquote>"The CIR doesn't reveal whether a company is innovative. It reveals whether a company knows what innovation means to them."</blockquote>

## The bureaucratic mirror

The standard CIR audit asks you to demonstrate that your project involved genuine scientific or technical uncertainty — that you didn't know, at the outset, whether the approach would work. This is harder than it sounds for most engineering teams.

Not because the work isn't real. But because organisations have optimised heavily for *the appearance of certainty*. Roadmaps, Gantt charts, quarterly deliverables — all of these create pressure to frame R&D as predictable execution, not genuine exploration.

The CIR requirement breaks that frame. And what I observe, consistently, is that the difficulty companies have answering the auditor's questions is a faithful reflection of the difficulty they'd have answering the same questions internally.<span class="fn">[2]</span>

## What distinguishes firms that manage it well

The companies that handle CIR documentation fluently — and there are some — share a specific quality: they have a shared internal language for uncertainty. They distinguish between *engineering* (applying known methods to known problems) and *research* (reducing uncertainty about whether something is even possible).

This distinction sounds obvious. It isn't, operationally. Most product roadmaps collapse both into a single category called "the sprint."

## A diagnostic, not a verdict

I don't think the CIR is a good proxy for innovation quality. Too much depends on sector, on history, on the specific interpretation of eligibility criteria in a given year.

But I do think the process of applying — and especially the experience of being audited — is diagnostic. It surfaces exactly the conceptual muddle that gets in the way of R&D strategy: the failure to distinguish hypothesis from specification, learning from delivery, uncertainty from risk.

Fix that muddle, and the CIR almost takes care of itself. More importantly, so does the innovation process it's meant to support.
